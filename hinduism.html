HINDUISM

Santana Dharma, popularly known as Hinduism, is the oldest surviving religion in the world. It is believed the divine revelations to ancient Rishis (sages and seers) resulted in the composition of the Vedas. The combined effort of all these sages collectively became the religion called Santana Dharma (basically means “Eternal Righteous Moral Order”). Unlike other religions, Hinduism did not begin at a certain point in time and has no one particular founder.

  • The Vedas are God’s words and the foundation of Santana Dharma
  • There is one all pervasive Brahman (God), who transcends space and time and manifests in many forms.
  • The Universe undergoes an endless cycle of creation, preservation, and dissolution.
  • Creation came out of the Creator and is part of the Divine
  • Under the laws of Karma, each individual creates his own destiny by their thoughts, words, and actions.
  • The soul reincarnates, evolving as it goes through many births and deaths, until all karmas are resolved, and moksha (liberation) is attained.
  • Through temple worship, rituals and personal devotion, Hindus create special personal relationship with a particular Deity of their choice.

What are different forms and types of rituals in Hinduism?

Rituals are means to an end which ultimately is to realize the Self through a pure heart. In the Hindu view, rituals help to concentrate the mind on worship, prayer, and meditation. In the advanced stages of religious life, the nature and extent of rituals may change to mainly include meditation and long periods of silence spent in contemplation of the Divine.

What does the chant Om represent?

Hindu scriptures declare that OM is the storehouse of mystic powers. It is the sound of Creation, and represents the sound of cosmic energy. Repetitive chanting of OM in accordance with the Vedic methods, generates mystic powers that leads one to concentrate, meditate, and finally get into a state of higher consciousness. The primordial sound OM is produced by combining the sounds of the letters A, U, and M. OM is also called the four element syllable, where the letters A,U,M are three elements, and the silence that follows the chanting to be the fourth element. The sound produced by the letter A represents the wakeful state, the letter U, the dream state, and M, the deep sleep state. Thus, OM represents the complete experiences of all the worlds, and the cosmic Brahman (ultimate reality).

What preparations does one make before entering a temple?

A temple is the home of God where devotees and the Divine commune. The vibration from the mantras (hymns) that are chanted, the prayers, and other rituals performed in the main hall all result in an environment that is vibrant with divine energy. As such, learned scholars advise devotees to look and feel their best when going to a temple so that they can experience the maximum benefit. It is best to bathe before and wear nice, modest, and clean clothing which would be suitable for sitting on the floor of the temple or prostrating in front of deities. It is common to see devotees washing their hands or feet before entering the temple, and shoes/slippers must be left outside at designated places. Inside the temple, a lot of devotees may bring fruit, flowers, milk, and other food items. While inside, it is important to maintain an atmosphere of silence, and only speak in soft tones when necessary. In essence, the temple environment gives devotees an opportunity to concentrate and perhaps even experience the Divine with Bhakti (devotion).

Is the caste system encouraged in Hindu scriptures?

The Varna system, or the caste system, is addressed as a positive factor in the Scriptures. The Varna system encourages division of labor within the society for efficient functioning, based on the natural tendencies of a person. In the Scriptures (the Bhagavad Gita) one’s Varna is determined by one’s own qualities, and not based on one’s birth into a family. Also, it should be addressed that the varna system as it is in the scriptures is a linear (not hierarchical) system. At the basic level, the 4 different Varna’s are Brahmin (thinkers who lead followers on a righteous path), Kshatriya (leaders or warriors), Vaishya (business and finance oriented people), and Sudras (unskilled workers). Most Hindus are strongly encouraged to educate and fight against the caste system as it is practiced today.

Why do Hindus worship cows?

Cows are gentle animals that give more than they take, and it is for this reason that Hindus honor and adore them. The cow provides nourishment to all and symbolizes the sustenance of life, endurance, maternal instincts, selfless service, and exemplifies the virtue of ahimsa.

Why do Hindus light lamps?

Light symbolizes knowledge and darkness symbolizes ignorance. Knowledge removes ignorance just as a light would remove darkness. Hence, Hindus bow down to the light which represents Knowledge and which is deemed to be the greatest of all forms of wealth. The traditional lamp has further significance. The oil represents desires, and the wick represents ego. When lit by spiritual knowledge, the desires (oil) gets slowly exhausted and ultimately even the wick (ego) burns out. The flame of the lamp is always burning upward, further symbolizing that one should always seeks higher ideals.

Why do some Hindus wear a tilak or bindi on their forehead?

Hindus wear a tilak/bindi, a red dot (by women) or an elongated line, on their foreheads between the two eyebrows. This is a special point known by various names, most commonly called the Third Eye, and it is said to be a major nerve in the human body. The red tilak also adds an aura of divinity to the person wearing it. A person wearing a tilak/bindi is implicitly asking to be aware of the Atma in them.

Why do Hindus fast on certain days?

Most devout Hindus fast regularly or on special occasions. They observe the fast by not eating at all, eating less, eating fruits, or just simple food (it varys from person to person and depends on the occasion of the fasting). Fasting is also called Upavaasa, with the word Upa meaning “being near” and Vaasa meaning “to stay.” Through the fasting process, Hindus stay near the Lord and contemplate on him instead of spending time on elaborate preparations of food. Fasting also helps to cultivate the ability to have self control, to have control over one’s senses, and to guide the mind to be peaceful. Obviously, it is unwise (and not recommended) to fast to the extent that one becomes weak or irritable.

What is Aarthi?

Aarthi is the waving of the lamp in circulatory motions several times. This signifies the completion of a pooja (prayer) ritual. It is usually accompanied by the ringing of bells, an Aarthi song or šloka chanting. This is therefor a very important ritual when the divine form of the deity, as well as the divine shakti of the deity, is revealed and experienced.

What is reincarnation in a Hindus perspective?

Simply put, reincarnation is the very embodiment of “cause and effect” relationships. Hindus believe that they impact their own future in this and future births through their actions and choices they make through free will. Accordingly, they will get to enjoy the positive results of their virtuous actions and the negative results of their negative actions until all their karmas are resolved so that they can attain moksha (salvation or freeing from the cycle of birth and death).

Why do Hindus not touch papers or books with their feet?

The custom of not stepping on educational tools is a frequent reminder of the high position of Knowledge in Hindu culture. This is the reason why books are worshipped (along with other objects, such as musical instruments) during Saraswati poojas. As to touching other people with one’s feet, in the Hindu view, all human beings have divinity (Atma) in them and are therefore living and breathing temples of the Lord. Therefore, touching others with the feet is similar to disrespecting the Atma within that person.
